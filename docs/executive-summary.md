# Executive summary: Quebec Bill 9 evidence baseline

**As of July 22, 2026**

## What the law establishes

Quebec's 2026 chapter 11 prohibits selling covered energy drinks to people under 16. A buyer must, when asked, prove that they are at least 16 using government or public-body photo identification showing name and date of birth. A seller must refuse when the document does not adequately establish identity. Most provisions enter into force on December 11, 2026. The remote and automated sales provisions depend on a future regulation under section 4.

## What current public evidence establishes

Official Quebec sources show that energy-drink use is not rare among youth and young adults. INSPQ reports that in 2022-2023 one youth in 16 consumed a sweetened caffeinated energy drink weekly and one in 100 daily. Institut de la statistique du Québec reports that 10 percent of people aged 15 to 24 consumed energy drinks at least weekly in 2020-2021.

INSPQ also warns that a sales prohibition may produce substitution to other caffeinated or sugary products or to informal markets. This is why the simulator now distinguishes transaction prevention from estimated consumption prevention.

Current public evidence does not directly establish the number of covered Quebec transactions, the share attempted by people under 16, how often retailers will request ID, lawful-adult rejection and abandonment rates, substitution, proxy purchase, retailer costs, or the data flows that future remote-sales rules will permit.

## What the simulator contributes

The simulator compares eight enforcement designs under common assumptions and reports:

- covered minor transactions prevented;
- substitution and estimated consumption prevention;
- lawful-adult false rejection and abandonment;
- higher-friction adult burdens;
- routine checks and full identity proofing;
- retained, centralized, transient, and biometric processing;
- routine and catastrophic breach exposure;
- fixed and variable implementation costs;
- ratios per prevented transaction or estimated prevented consumption.

The model does not select a universal winner. Different methods can deliver similar nominal prevention while producing very different access, privacy, and cost profiles.

## Most important conclusion of v0.2

The immediate research priority is not a more complicated simulator. It is direct measurement of the inputs that drive policy conclusions.

The project now ranks those gaps using sensitivity analysis and evidence coverage. Priority fieldwork should focus on:

1. transaction volume and minor share;
2. realistic ID challenge rates;
3. adult abandonment and false rejection;
4. circumvention and substitution;
5. retailer time and implementation cost;
6. ID access and accessible alternatives;
7. actual document, biometric, token, and retention data flows;
8. the content of future section 4 regulations.

## Decision-use warning

Version 0.2 is suitable for research design, assumption stress testing, source auditing, and identifying what must be measured. It is not suitable for forecasting the effects of Bill 9 or recommending a specific verification vendor without further evidence.
